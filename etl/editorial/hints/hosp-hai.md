- HAI data comes from the CDC National Healthcare Safety Network (NHSN), not from CMS claims — hospitals report infections directly to NHSN as part of CMS quality reporting requirements. This means HAI data uses clinical definitions, not billing codes.
- The Standardized Infection Ratio (SIR) compares observed infections to a baseline prediction derived from 2015 national data. An SIR of 1.0 means the hospital matched the 2015 national baseline; an SIR below 1.0 indicates fewer infections than predicted. The baseline year is not updated annually, so national improvements over time cause more hospitals to appear "better than expected."
- Six measures are reported: CLABSI (central line bloodstream infections), CAUTI (catheter-associated urinary tract infections), SSI for colon surgery, SSI for abdominal hysterectomy, MRSA bacteremia, and C. difficile (CDI). Not all hospitals perform all procedures, so some measures are not applicable.
- Hospitals with very low device-days or procedure counts will have predicted infections below 1.0, making SIR calculation unreliable — these are suppressed rather than reported.
- Surveillance definitions for HAIs have changed over time (e.g., NHSN updated CAUTI definitions in 2015), which affects trend comparisons across reporting periods.
