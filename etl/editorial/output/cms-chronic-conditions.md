## Overview

The Medicare Specific Chronic Conditions by County dataset reports the prevalence of 21 chronic conditions among Medicare fee-for-service (FFS) beneficiaries, aggregated at the county level. Published by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) through the Chronic Conditions Data Warehouse (CCW) program, this dataset covers conditions including diabetes, hypertension, heart failure, COPD, Alzheimer's disease and related dementias, depression, cancer, and HIV/AIDS, among others.

Conditions are identified using CCW reference period algorithms applied to Medicare FFS claims. Each algorithm requires a specific number of qualifying diagnosis codes within a defined lookback window (typically 1â€“2 years depending on the condition). The dataset enables county-level comparisons of chronic disease burden across the Medicare FFS population, identification of geographic hotspots for specific conditions, and analysis of multi-morbidity patterns. Over 60% of Medicare FFS beneficiaries have two or more chronic conditions; the individual condition prevalence rates are not mutually exclusive, as the same beneficiary is counted under every condition for which they qualify.

## Join Strategy

Each record in this dataset is keyed by FIPS county code, a 5-digit zero-padded string formed by concatenating the 2-digit state FIPS code with the 3-digit county FIPS code (e.g., `06037` for Los Angeles County, California). CareGraph joins this dataset to county entity pages by matching on the FIPS code directly. No normalization beyond zero-padding is required, as the source data uses standard 5-digit FIPS codes. The chronic condition prevalence rates appear on county pages alongside other county-level metrics, enabling users to view disease burden in the context of hospital quality, ACO participation, and demographic data for the same geography.

## Known Limitations

- **Medicare Advantage exclusion.** Prevalence rates reflect Medicare FFS beneficiaries only. Medicare Advantage enrollees â€” who exceed 50% of Medicare beneficiaries in many markets â€” are excluded entirely. The FFS population in high-MA-penetration counties may be systematically different from the overall Medicare population (e.g., sicker, older, or lower-income), biasing prevalence estimates in unpredictable directions.
- **Small-cell suppression.** Counties with fewer than 11 beneficiaries diagnosed with a given condition have that condition's prevalence rate suppressed to protect patient privacy. This disproportionately affects small rural counties and less-common conditions such as HIV/AIDS and hepatitis.
- **Claims-based identification.** Conditions are identified from administrative claims, not clinical records or electronic health records. Under-coding and under-diagnosis cause prevalence to be systematically underestimated, particularly for conditions like depression and Alzheimer's disease where diagnostic coding practices vary widely across providers and regions.
- **CCW algorithm lookback windows.** Each condition uses a specific CCW reference period algorithm requiring a defined number of qualifying claims within a 1â€“2 year lookback window. Beneficiaries who have a condition but did not generate sufficient qualifying claims during the reference period are not flagged, further underestimating true prevalence.
- **Reporting lag.** Claims data requires a run-out period for processing and adjudication. The dataset typically reflects conditions identified from claims filed 12â€“18 months prior to the publication date.
- **Non-mutually-exclusive counts.** Individual condition prevalence rates cannot be summed to derive a total disease burden figure, as multi-morbid beneficiaries are counted under every qualifying condition.

## Data Quality Notes

- **Suppressed values.** Suppressed prevalence rates (cells with fewer than 11 beneficiaries) appear as missing or null values rather than zeros. During ETL, these are preserved as nulls to avoid conflating suppression with zero prevalence. Users should not interpret missing prevalence for a condition as absence of that condition in the county.
- **FIPS code encoding.** FIPS codes are ingested and stored as zero-padded 5-character strings. Source data occasionally drops leading zeros (e.g., `6037` instead of `06037`); the ETL normalizes all FIPS codes to 5-digit strings during the key normalization step.
- **Prevalence rate precision.** Prevalence rates are expressed as percentages with varying decimal precision in the source data. CareGraph rounds displayed values to one decimal place for consistency across county pages.
- **Condition flag consistency.** The 21 conditions tracked use heterogeneous CCW algorithms with different lookback periods, claim count thresholds, and qualifying diagnosis code sets. This means sensitivity and specificity vary by condition â€” diabetes prevalence is relatively well-captured, while depression and dementia prevalence are known to be significantly underestimated due to inconsistent coding practices.
